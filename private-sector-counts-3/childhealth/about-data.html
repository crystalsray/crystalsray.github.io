About the Data

The data used in this tool come from the Demographic Health Surveys (DHS), currently managed by ICF International. For a detailed explanation of the indicators and surveys of the DHS program, go to dhsprogram.com/data.

Health Source Counts uses surveys from 24 USAID maternal and child survival priority countries from 2006-2016, using the most recent DHS data available for each country. A full list of all surveys used and countries is included below.

The countries are divided into four regions: seven in Asia (South Asia, Southeast Asia, and Near East); nine in East and Southern Africa; six in West and Central Africa; and two (Haiti and Yemen) in an “other” category.

Definition of Terms

Illnesses - Data are reported by mothers who were asked if their children under age five experienced ARI, fever, or diarrhea in the two weeks before the interview. These data do not report whether children recently had pneumonia or malaria because both of these illnesses must be confirmed in a laboratory. Instead, the DHS reports whether or not children had recent symptoms of ARI as a proxy for pneumonia, and fever as a proxy for malaria. ARI is defined as a reported cough with chest-related rapid or difficult breathing.

Treatment sources - The providers to whom the caregivers of children sick with ARI, fever, or go for treatment or advice are grouped into private sector, public sector, and “other” sources. These three broad categories are represented by “consolidated sources.” The tool also presents values for unconsolidated treatment sources, including no care sought outside the home, presenting a more granular view.

Private sector consists of:

  • private clinics, hospitals, or clinicians, along with non-governmental organizations and faith-based organizations
  • private pharmacies
  • private shops and markets

Public sector includes:

  • public facilities such as government clinics, government hospitals, government health centers
  • public community health workers

Other sources are informal providers and include traditional healers, friends, and family members.

When the tool focuses on treatment sources for children with at least one of the three diseases, the categories are modified to account for potential overlap in treatment sources. The categories become: private only, public only, both public and private, other only, and no care sought outside home.
